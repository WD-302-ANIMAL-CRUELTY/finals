When choosing to develop or use new, untested ingredients in their cosmetic products, companies conduct tests on animals to assess the safety of these new ingredients. However, animal tests have scientific limitations because animals often respond differently than humans when exposed to the same chemicals, leading to inaccurate estimates of real-world hazards to people.

Cosmetic testing on animals has been a practice for decades now and to this day, there are still animals who suffer in the testing of shampoo, mascara, and other cosmetic products. Any ingredient used in a cosmetic also falls under this definition and products normally labeled as cosmetics are classified as drugs when a medical claim is made. Often times we always forget that the items we use on a daily basis might have been tested on animals. Most frequently used are mice, rats, rabbits, and guinea pigs which can cause irritations to severe damages or even worseâ€“death. Although humans can benefit from successful animal research, the pain and death of animals are not worth the possible human benefits.

Rabbits, mice, guinea pigs, and rats are used in a variety of invasive experiments and these tests can result in immense pain, distress, blindness, swollen eyes, sore and bleeding skin, internal bleeding, organ damage, birth defects, convulsions and even death in the animals. This may involve the following:

Skin and eye irritation tests where chemicals are rubbed onto the shaved skin or dripped into the eyes of restrained rabbits, without any pain relief.

Tests that deliver doses of chemical substances to mice through repeated force-feeding. These tests last weeks or months so researchers can look for signs of general illness or specific health hazards such as cancer or birth defects.

Widely condemned "lethal dose" tests, in which rats are forced to swallow large amounts of chemicals to determine the dose that causes death.

At the end of some tests, the animals are killed, normally by asphyxiation, neck-breaking or decapitation and pain relief is not provided.

Replacing cruelty testing does not mean halting medical progress. Rather, replacing with humane alternatives would increase the consistency and humaneness of research. Fortunately, alternative approaches are becoming more common and thousands of products are now on the market that are made from ingredients that have a long history of being healthy and do not need further testing.

1. Human volunteers.
Human volunteers' studies are far more applicable to the general public than animal tests. Human volunteers are clearly at risk, according to critics of this choice. Advances in technology, on the other hand, enable researchers to test chemicals on humans with minimal risk.

2. Computer Models
Computer models remove the need for animal research and do not necessitate the use of human subjects. These models are also cost-effective, since they can be used over and over again without wasting money.

3. In Vitro Testing
To assess the toxicity or irritation potential of particular chemicals, this approach uses real human cells. One method of in-vitro testing produced by Harvard researchers, for example, entails the development of microchips containing human cells. On these microchips, various types of human cells are used to mimic the structure and function of particular organs or structures in the human body, such as the skin. Researchers may use these chips to see how particular body systems respond to a new chemical, eliminating the need for animal testing. In the cosmetics industry, the cells will be used to monitor reactions to specific substances by simulating the function of human skin.
